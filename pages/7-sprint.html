SPRINT

Sprints are the heartbeat of Scrum, where ideas are turned into value. During a Sprint, the Scrum Team works to complete a set of product backlog items (PBIs) that contribute to the Sprint Goal. Each Sprint may be considered a short project. All the work necessary to achieve the Product Goal, including Sprint Planning, Daily Scrums, Sprint Review, and Sprint Retrospective, happen within Sprints.

DURATION

Sprints are fixed length events of one month or less to create consistency. The team must define the Sprint's length before starting it, which means that it must be done before the Sprint Planning meeting. The length of a Sprint is decided collaboratively by the Scrum Team, considering factors like product complexity, stakeholder feedback, historical data, and organizational constraints. This decision-making process involves open discussions and consensus-building within the team. The goal is to select a Sprint duration that allows for consistent delivery of value while promoting collaboration, transparency, and adaptability within the Scrum framework.

Once a Sprint begins, its duration remains fixed and cannot be extended or shortened. The Sprint's duration remains consistent throughout the project. Regardless of completed work, the Sprint ends when the time-boxed period concludes. This time-boxing aspect ensures predictability, consistency, and focused effort within the development process, preventing scope creep and encouraging the delivery of incremental value. When a Sprint's horizon is too long the Sprint Goal may become invalid, complexity may rise, and risk may increase. Shorter Sprints can be employed to generate more learning cycles and limit risk of cost and effort to a smaller time frame. The team experiments and adapts as necessary, evaluating its effectiveness over time.

RULES

No changes are made that would endanger the Sprint Goal

  • The Scrum Team ensures that any changes made during the Sprint do not jeopardize the Sprint Goal, which is the overarching objective for the Sprint.
  • Changes that align with the Sprint Goal or enhance the value of the product increment may be considered, but those that deviate from the goal or introduce unnecessary risk are avoided.

Quality does not decrease

  • Despite any changes or adaptations made during the Sprint, the Scrum Team maintains a focus on delivering high-quality work.
  • Quality standards and practices, such as code reviews, testing, and adherence to the Definition of Done, are upheld to ensure that the product increment meets the expected level of quality.

The Product Backlog is refined as needed

  • Throughout the Sprint, the Product Backlog is refined as new insights emerge, requirements evolve, or priorities shift.
  • The Product Owner (PO) collaborates with stakeholders and the Scrum Team to update the Product Backlog, ensuring that it remains current, prioritized, and aligned with the overall product vision.

Scope may be clarified and renegotiated with the PO as more is learned

  • As the Scrum Team gains a deeper understanding of the work and its requirements during the Sprint, the scope may need to be clarified or adjusted.
  • The PO works closely with the team to review and refine the scope as necessary, ensuring that it reflects the latest insights, stakeholder feedback, and market conditions.
  • If significant changes are required, the scope may be renegotiated with the PO to ensure alignment with project objectives and constraints.

KEY CHARACTERISTICS

  • A new Sprint starts immediately after the conclusion of the previous Sprint. This establishes a regular rhythm for development, providing stakeholders with consistent updates and product increments, aiding in better planning and decision-making.
  • Sprints enable predictability by ensuring that the Scrum Team inspects and adapts toward the Product Goal and Sprint Goal each Sprint.

    Product Goal - The Product Goal describes a future state of the product which can serve as a target for the Scrum Team to plan against. The Product Goal is in the Product Backlog. The rest of the Product Backlog emerges to define “what” will fulfill the Product Goal. The Product Goal is the long-term objective for the Scrum Team. They must fulfill (or abandon) one objective before taking on the next.

    Sprint Goal - The Sprint Goal is the single objective for the Sprint. Although the Sprint Goal is a commitment by the Developers, it provides flexibility in terms of the exact work needed to achieve it. The Sprint Goal also creates coherence and focus, encouraging the Scrum Team to work together rather than on separate initiatives.

EMPIRICISM

Empiricism is the most important practice for the Scrum Team to embrace during the Sprint. There is a lot unknown in complex environments. By embracing empiricism the team will learn by doing and will continue to improve as you navigate complex work.
Forward looking decisions can only be made by the Scrum Team based on what has already happened.
Various practices exist to forecast progress, like burn-downs, burn-ups, or cumulative flows. While proven useful, these do not replace the importance of empiricism.

PREREQUISITES FOR STARTING A SPRINT

Prior to initiating the initial Sprint, two critical prerequisites must be met.
1. Firstly, there should be a sufficient number of PBIs ready for commencement, each adequately refined and detailed for Developer action.
2. Secondly, the establishment of a cross-functional Scrum Team is essential, comprising the PO, Developers, and Scrum Master, possessing all requisite skills for product development.

MANAGING WORK CHANGES DURING SPRINT

If any stakeholder requests additional work from the Developers during a Sprint, it is crucial for them to promptly inform the PO. The PO is responsible for managing the Product Backlog and determining the priority and sequence of work. The Sprint's focus is based on items chosen during Sprint Planning from the Product Backlog. Introducing new tasks during a Sprint may disrupt the team's focus and commitment to the Sprint Goal.
Therefore, it is the responsibility of the PO to handle such requests and decide whether the new work should be included in the Product Backlog for future Sprints. While changing the Sprint scope is feasible, it necessitates discussions between the PO and Developers to assess whether such changes hinder the Sprint Goal.

When there's surplus time available for Developers within the Sprint, it's advisable to jointly involve both Developers and the PO in selecting additional tasks. Developers possess insights into remaining capacity and technical feasibility, while the PO holds authority in prioritizing based on work value and importance. Through collaborative effort, both parties can decide which additional items from the Product Backlog can be addressed during the ongoing Sprint.

WHAT HAPPENS TO UNFINISHED ITEMS AT THE END OF A SPRINT

It's acceptable to have unfinished items, as the Sprint Backlog serves as an estimate.
In cases where it becomes evident that not all forecasted PBIs can be completed within the Sprint, it's imperative for the Developers and the PO to engage in a discussion. This collaborative effort aims to gain a deeper understanding of the situation, assess current progress, and adjust plans if necessary.
Potential actions may include negotiating Sprint scope or reordering the Product Backlog. While the Scrum Master can facilitate this discussion, the primary dialogue should occur between the Developers, and the PO. Unfinished items revert to the Product Backlog, while events remain unaffected.

SPRINT CANCELLATION

Sprint cancellation in Scrum refers to the decision to prematurely end a Sprint before its planned duration. A Sprint could be cancelled if the Sprint Goal becomes obsolete, due to various reasons, such as significant changes in market conditions, company strategy, or technology. The decision to cancel a Sprint should be made in exceptional circumstances when continuing the Sprint would not be in the best interest of the project or the product. It requires careful consideration and communication among the Scrum Team and stakeholders to ensure alignment and minimize disruptions to the development process.

Only the PO has the authority to cancel the Sprint, since they are responsible for maximizing the value of the product and determining the release strategy. If the decision is made to cancel a Sprint, the PO communicates this to the Scrum Team during the Sprint Review or as soon as the decision is made. The team then stops work on the Sprint backlog items and holds a Sprint Retrospective to reflect on the reasons for the cancellation and identify lessons learned. The PO may work with stakeholders to determine the next steps, such as re-prioritizing the Product Backlog or adjusting the Sprint duration.

COMMON ANTIPATTERNS

  •  Continually rolling over work from one Sprint to the next
    Teams that do not plan their work to fit into the Sprint find themselves pushing work into the next Sprint.
  •  Frequently changing the Sprint length
    As the product evolves, it may be appropriate to either lengthen or shorten the Sprint. However, frequently changing the length of the Sprint may indicate that the team is going back and forth between a desire to have less frequent meetings (Sprint Planning, Sprint Review and Sprint Retrospective) and their realization that they need more frequent feedback from stakeholders.
  •  Specialized Sprints
    Specialized Sprints (such as design, analysis or testing Sprints) generally indicate that something is wrong in the team's understanding of Scrum. For example:

    Sprint 0 - Some teams create a “Sprint 0” which is a Sprint before the formal start of the project. When a team suggests having a Sprint 0, it may be because they feel they must create some artifacts (such as a Product Backlog and the Definition of Done), or resolve some procedural or technical questions (such as what tools to use in developing the product) before Sprinting. However, the essence of Scrum is to be able to start with complex problems and to incrementally and iteratively solve them. There is no such thing as “Sprint 0,” it's just a “Sprint.”

    Hardening Sprint - Some teams create a “hardening” Sprint which is dedicated to improving the quality of the product. If the team feels it's necessary to have a “hardening” Sprint, it's often because they haven't taken care in creating, evolving and conforming to their Definition of Done. They typically take on too much work and don't have the time to create a high-quality result. There should not be “hardening” Sprints. Instead, in early Sprints the Definition of Done may be less stringent and over the course of many Sprints, the Definition of Done may become increasingly stringent, forcing later Sprints to have higher quality expectations rather than abruptly creating a Sprint to rectify the quality issues.

  •  Running Sprints as mini-waterfall projects
    The beginning of the Sprint is used for solution development. Near the end of the Sprint timeframe, all of the work goes into a testing phrase.
  •  Putting the entire Sprint Backlog in “doing” when the Sprint starts
    Often, teams use a board to visualize the state of each Sprint Backlog item. At a minimum, these states are “To Do,” “In Progress,” “Blocked” or “Done.” This antipattern results when Developers select the PBIs they intend to work on at the beginning of the Sprint and put them all in the “doing/in progress” state.

TIPS FOR STRONG SPRINTS

Breaking the antipatterns listed above help create strong and effective Sprints. Consider the following tips:

  •  A two-week Sprint may be a good starting point for determining the Sprint length. If PBIs cannot be made small enough to fit into a two-week Sprint or Sprint Goals cannot be defined so that they can be achieved in two weeks, consider lengthening the Sprint. Shorter Sprints are beneficial because they provide opportunities for more frequent feedback and adaptation.
  •  Keep the length of the Sprint as consistent as possible. Sometimes teams increase the length of the Sprint during vacation periods, often to keep their “velocity” consistent across Sprints. However, velocity is a poor measure of value creation. Teams should simply reduce the amount of work they forecast during periods of high absenteeism.
