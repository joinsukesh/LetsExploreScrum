ESTIMATIONS - SIZING

In traditional project management, time estimation is typically assigned to each individual task. However, in Agile methodologies, this approach isn't favored due to the considerable effort involved, and the tendency for resulting estimates to be inaccurate. The larger the task being estimated, the more challenging it becomes for us as humans to accurately gauge its size. So, what's the alternative? The concept of relative sizing comes into play.

RELATIVE SIZING

Relative sizing (or just Sizing in short) is a technique used in Agile project management to estimate the size or complexity of tasks or user stories relative to one another, rather than assigning specific time durations or absolute values to each task. It involves comparing different tasks or user stories based on their perceived complexity, effort, or size.

Sizing helps the Scrum Team understand the relative size of each item, which aids in planning and prioritization. It allows the team to forecast how much work they can complete within a given Sprint and helps in determining the team's velocity.

Scrum does not prescribe any rules about estimates and there are a lot of possibilities, but the most popular measure unit is the Story Point, which is an Agile alternative to hours and days. And the most used estimation technique is Planning Poker.

STORY POINTS

Story points are a unit of measure used to estimate the overall effort required to implement a user story. Instead of estimating in hours, team members assign story points based on factors like complexity, uncertainty, and effort required.

The semi-Fibonacci series is often used for sizing tasks during the estimation process, particularly when estimating user stories using story points. It is a modified version of the Fibonacci sequence.
The Fibonacci sequence starts with 0 and 1, and each subsequent number is the sum of the two preceding numbers (0, 1, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, and so on).

In the semi-Fibonacci scale, the sequence follows a similar pattern but with some modifications. Instead of strictly adhering to the Fibonacci sequence, it incorporates some increments or deviations to better suit the needs of the team. For example, it might include values like 0, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 20, 40, and so on.

This modified scale is used for sizing tasks because it provides a more nuanced range of values than a linear scale, allowing for better differentiation between the sizes or complexities of tasks. By using a semi-Fibonacci scale, teams can more accurately assess and prioritize tasks based on their relative sizes, making it easier to plan and allocate resources effectively in Agile project management.

PLANNING POKER

Planning Poker is a collaborative estimation technique used in Agile and Scrum methodologies to estimate the relative size or effort required to complete a specific task, user story, or feature. It is typically employed during Sprint Planning sessions to facilitate the process of determining the effort involved in completing the items in the Product Backlog.

Here's how Planning Poker typically works:

 Preparation: Before the planning poker session, the Product Owner (PO) prepares a list of user stories or backlog items to be estimated. These are typically written on index cards or displayed on a screen.

 Participants: Estimates in Scrum are made by those doing the work, which in this case would be the Developers. The Developers have the best understanding of the effort and complexity involved in completing a Product Backlog item (PBI) and are, therefore, best positioned to provide accurate estimates. While the PO may provide input and guidance, ultimately, it is up to the Developers to estimate the work. The Scrum Master can support this process by facilitating discussions and helping the team to reach a shared understanding of the work involved. The team should ideally consist of individuals with diverse perspectives and expertise relevant to the tasks being estimated.

 Card Decks: Each participant is given a set of cards with values representing different levels of effort or complexity. These values are typically based on the semi-Fibonacci sequence (e.g., 0, 1, 2, 3, 5, 8, 13, 20, 40, etc.) The cards are usually numbered or labeled to correspond to these values.

 Estimation Process: The PO presents one backlog item at a time to the team. The team discusses the item and asks clarifying questions if needed. Once everyone understands the scope of the item, each participant selects a card from their deck representing their estimate of the effort required to complete the item.

 Reveal and Discussion: Once everyone has selected a card, all participants reveal their chosen card simultaneously. If there is a wide range of estimates, participants discuss their reasoning and any differing perspectives on the item. This discussion helps the team align their understanding and reach a consensus on the estimate. If, for instance, someone estimated a 3 and the other one an 8, these members will have to show their reasons. After they discuss the team goes to another round of estimation until consensus is reached.

 Repeat: The process is repeated for each backlog item until all items have been estimated. If there are particularly complex items or significant discrepancies in estimates, additional discussion and rounds of estimation may be necessary.


The goal of Planning Poker is to reach a consensus estimate for each backlog item. This consensus estimate represents the team's collective understanding of the effort involved and provides a basis for prioritizing and planning the work in the upcoming sprint.

HOW DO YOU START ESTIMATING

As a rule of thumb, during the first Planning Poker session, the team will select the smallest PBI, which is a really easy one that the team could get done in a few days. And then give it a 2. Why give it a 2 and not a 1? Because we leave room for smaller items that can come up in the future. So after giving the first user story a 2, the team can estimate the remaining ones in comparison to that one.

ONLY A TEAM METRIC

A story point is a team metric, and we can never compare the productivity of teams using story points.
The allocation of story points in Agile teams is influenced by factors such as team expertise and task complexity. For instance, Team A, familiar with augmented reality, might assign a backlog item 5 points, while another team less experienced in the same area might assign 13 points due to perceived risk. Similarly, different technology domains like blockchain may lead to varied estimates; Team B might rate a task at 8 points, whereas Team A might assign 40 points. This illustrates that story point allocation isn't indicative of team speed but reflects the unique challenges and proficiencies of each team.

CONCLUSION

Sizing is an important aspect of Scrum because it helps in creating realistic Sprint plans, managing expectations, and providing transparency to stakeholders regarding the team's capacity and progress. However, it is crucial to understand that story points or sizing estimates are not fixed values but rather relative measures that evolve as the team gains more understanding and experience with the project.
