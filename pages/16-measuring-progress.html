MEASURING PROGRESS

In order to deliver functioning product increments on a regular basis, Scrum requires that teams track and manage progress throughout the project. Various practices exist to forecast progress, like burndowns, burnups, or cumulative flows. While proving useful, these do not replace the importance of empiricism.

BURNDOWN CHART

A burndown chart is a visual depiction of the remaining work within a Sprint, plotted over time. It offers a clear indication of the team's progress and highlights any deviations from the planned schedule. As tasks are completed, the chart gradually diminishes to zero by the end of the designated time frame.
While not mandated by Scrum, burndown charts serve as valuable tools for monitoring progress and ensuring transparency.

The idea behind the burndown chart is to gradually reduce the remaining work, starting from the top and progressing downwards until the goal is achieved. Typically, this goal involves delivering the Product or Sprint Backlog. Thus, the initial workload is represented by the total points assigned to the Product or Sprint Backlog.

How to create a Sprint burndown chart

The Y-axis is for effort, generally measured in story points (SPs). The X-axis is for time, generally measured in days, hours, Sprints.
The Scrum Team could use any other units here, whatever they find useful.

The ideal effort (blue line) represents what the burndown chart will look like if the estimated work is completed on time.
Initially, the total estimated work is represented at the top of the Y-axis, serving as the starting point.
For example, if the team estimates that they will complete 20 SPs, the number 20 is plotted at the top of the Y-axis. A finishing point is marked on the X-axis, representing the point where all work is estimated to be completed. A straight diagonal line from the starting point to the finishing point represents the expected “burndown” slope if the work is completed on time.

Each day, the team updates and plots the progress against the work remaining. These updates represent the remaining effort (yellow line) compared to the ideal effort.

For example, if the Sprint Backlog initially comprises 20 SPs, 20 is plotted at X=0 and Y=20, indicating the starting workload. If, after the first day, 19 points remain, another point is marked at X=1 and Y=19.

Ideally, the remaining effort would line up with the ideal effort. In reality, some work will be marked below the ideal effort line (indicating that the team is ahead of schedule), and other work will be marked above the line (indicating that the team is behind schedule).

By comparing the estimated and actual work lines, the team assesses whether the project is on track or behind schedule.

For a Product burndown chart, the amount of work remaining in the Product Backlog over time, is represented on the Y-axis by the number of Product Backlog Items, and the Sprints on the X-axis.

Advantages of using a burndown chart

  • Provides a clear visual representation, aiding in better comprehension and retention of information.
  • Enhances transparency within the team, as the chart's visibility prompts regular performance evaluations and updates to work estimates.
  • Mitigates risk by providing daily feedback on schedule, actual work, and estimated work, enabling swift issue identification and adjustment.
  • Saves time by offering a faster and more straightforward method of tracking progress compared to sorting through emails and other documents for project status updates.

Disadvantages of using a burndown chart

  • Limited ability to convey the entire story, as it is hard to determine whether changes result from adding or subtracting SPs or completing backlog items.
  • Its accuracy depends heavily on precise data, making it challenging to interpret deviations from the ideal effort line. For instance, if the actual line falls below or above the expected effort line, it could mean the team is ahead or behind schedule? Or, it could be that the team overestimated or underestimated the work?
  • Overestimation of time requirements can lead to inflated expectations.
  • The chart fails to reflect how close the team is to completing the work or which backlog items have been finished.
  • It poorly represents effort when faced with dynamic changes in the Product Backlog, undermining its effectiveness in reflecting progress accurately.

BURNUP CHART

A burnup chart visually represents a team's progress throughout a project's duration. It provides project managers and teams with a clear overview of workload progression and whether the project is on track for completion.
Unlike the downward progression of a burndown chart, the burnup chart moves upward from bottom to top, which explains its name.

The chart typically displays two main lines: one representing the total planned project work (blue line) and the other tracking the work completed up to the current point (orange line). By comparing the team's completed work with the overall planned workload, one can gauge their efficiency and make more accurate estimates regarding the remaining work's duration.

Burnup charts prioritize showcasing cumulative achievements over time rather than emphasizing specific deadlines.

The Y-axis represents either the total count of SPs or the workload yet to be finished, while the X-axis denotes the Sprint number or the timeframe.
At the top, a blue line indicates the total intended SPs to be achieved by the project's conclusion.
A dotted black line marks the projected completion of SPs.
The orange line tracks the actual number of SPs completed.

Here are some important points to consider when analyzing a burnup chart:

  • Changes in the blue line indicate additions or removals of work from the project.
  • Alterations in the black line signal adjustments to the baseline plan regarding the amount of work planned for each Sprint.
  • If the orange line falls below the black line, it indicates that the project is lagging behind schedule.
  • Conversely, if the orange line surpasses the black line, it suggests that the project is ahead of schedule.

Advantages of a burnup chart

  • Provides a clear and visual representation of the progress made during the Sprint, allowing everyone involved to see how the work is progressing towards the Sprint goal.
  • By comparing the planned work against the actual progress, the burnup chart helps teams identify any deviations or obstacles early in the Sprint. This allows them to take corrective actions promptly, such as reprioritizing tasks or adjusting the Sprint scope.
  • The chart helps teams forecast whether they are on track to complete all planned work by the end of the Sprint. By extrapolating the current velocity, teams can estimate whether they will finish all committed items or if adjustments need to be made to ensure successful Sprint completion.
  • Visualizing progress can be motivating for team members as they see tasks being completed and the Sprint goal getting closer. It also helps them stay focused and encourages them to work collaboratively towards achieving the Sprint goal.

CUMULATIVE FLOW DIAGRAM

A cumulative flow diagram (CFD) is a graphical representation illustrating the progress of various tasks within a project, showing their transition from the initial "to-do" stage to completion.
The Y-axis indicates the number of tasks being actively worked on by the team throughout the project, while the X-axis denotes the passage of time.

The graph is divided into three color-coded bars namely: To Do tasks, In Progress tasks & Completed tasks.

This graph depicts three bars within the same visual, each representing the quantity of tasks at different stages. the large graph is always correct since the sum of the number of tasks in the three bars always adds up to the whole.

As new tasks are added to or created in the project, the "To Do" bar grows.
When these tasks are picked by the team, the increase moves to the "In Progress" bar.
Finally, when these tasks are completed, the increase moves to the "Completed" bar.
This way, at every moment, the team can instantly have a high-level overview of how the project and its constituent tasks are progressing.

Advantages of CFD

Cumulative flow diagrams are effective tools for providing a comprehensive overview of a project's progress through various stages. They simplify data into an easy-to-understand format, allowing teams to assess task completion rates and potential bottlenecks. By analyzing the diagram, project managers can identify problem areas in workflow or task execution, enabling them to address bottlenecks and optimize processes for improved efficiency.

SPRINT VELOCITY

To meet our goals and consistently deliver high-quality products within tight timelines, it's crucial to understand the team's capacity to complete tasks within each Sprint. This insight allows us to more accurately predict project completion timelines and ensures that we allocate resources optimally to support the team's efforts.

By analyzing the team's historical Sprint performance, we can estimate their future capacity, which is commonly referred to as Sprint velocity in Agile development. With this knowledge, we can effectively plan projects and anticipate the workload for upcoming Sprints.
Additionally, it enables us to better forecast resource requirements and project completion efforts. Sharing our Sprint velocity estimate with stakeholders provides them with a clearer understanding of when to expect product delivery.

How to estimate Sprint velocity

Assessing past achievements is crucial for predicting future work completion. To obtain an accurate average of completed tasks, it's advisable to review past Sprints, at least the last three.

Estimating Sprint velocity involves averaging the completed work from previous Sprints, measured in SPs. For instance, if Sprint 1, 2, and 3 yielded 20, 30, and 40 SPs respectively, the average Sprint velocity would be (20 + 30 + 40)/3, equating to 30 SPs.

It's vital to only include SPs from "Done" work, i.e., stories meeting the Definition of Done criteria.

This average Sprint velocity of 30 SPs can then guide future Sprint planning. For instance, if there are 150 SPs remaining in the project, it suggests the team may need around five more Sprints to complete the project.

However, this estimation is contingent on factors like team size, project complexity, and scope remaining consistent. Teams typically encounter fluctuations from one Sprint to another. Nevertheless, Sprint velocity estimation serves as a useful starting point for gauging the team's capacity.

For Agile teams new to the process, lacking past Sprint data, completing a few initial Sprints while tracking completed SPs is necessary. This data accumulation enables the calculation of an average Sprint velocity.

It's essential to remember that Sprint velocity is an estimation and shouldn't be solely relied upon to predict what the team will deliver. Actual completed SPs may deviate from the estimated figure.
