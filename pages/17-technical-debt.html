TECHNICAL DEBT

Technical debt is a metaphor used to explain to non-technical stakeholders the repercussions of subpar software development practices. It encompasses issues like code decay, architectural deterioration, and shortcomings in design documentation and testing.
Essentially, high technical debt means that the code is difficult to work with and changing it comes with significant costs. This contradicts the Agile principle of being responsive to change.

Technical debt can obscure the true state of a product by concealing underlying issues or flawed designs, potentially impeding a team's productivity as resources are redirected to rectify earlier suboptimal decisions. Neglecting technical debt can escalate maintenance expenses, diminish productivity, and compromise the team's capacity to deliver value to customers efficiently

The Scrum Guide doesn't explicitly address technical debt, but it emphasizes sustainable pace and continuous improvement. While it is ideal for a Scrum Team to minimize technical debt, it is not realistic to expect a Scrum Team to have no technical debt. The Scrum Team should prioritize and address technical debt when it becomes necessary and impacts the ability to deliver value.

There's no fixed timeframe for addressing technical debt in Scrum. It's up to the team to decide when and how to tackle it, considering their goals and standards for the product.
One way to limit the accumulation of technical debt is by having an appropriate Definition of Done. The Definition of Done sets the quality criteria that must be met for a Product Backlog item to be considered complete. By continuously refining and improving the Definition of Done, the Scrum Team ensures that each Increment is thoroughly tested, integrated, and meets the required quality standards, reducing the chances of accumulating technical debt.
