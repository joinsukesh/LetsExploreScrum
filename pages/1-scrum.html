WHAT IS SCRUM

The term "Scrum" is inspired by the scrum formation in the sport of rugby, where the team collaborates to move the ball forward. Similarly, in Scrum, the team works together to move the product forward.
Scrum is an agile framework for developing, delivering, and sustaining complex products. The Scrum framework is purposefully incomplete, only defining the parts required to implement Scrum theory. Scrum is built upon by the collective intelligence of the people using it. Rather than provide people with detailed instructions, the rules of Scrum guide their relationships and interactions.
Various processes, techniques, and methods can be employed within the framework. Scrum wraps around existing practices or renders them unnecessary. Scrum makes visible the relative efficacy of current management, environment, and work techniques so that improvements can be made.

THE SCRUM THEORY

In complex environments where there is uncertainty and incomplete information, making a decision is often preferable to not making any decision at all. This is because inaction can lead to delays and missed opportunities.
Scrum is founded on empiricism and lean thinking. Empiricism asserts that knowledge comes from experience and making decisions based on what is observed. Lean thinking reduces waste and focuses on the essentials.
Scrum employs an iterative, incremental approach to optimize predictability and to control risk. Scrum engages groups of people who collectively have all the skills and expertise to do the work and share or acquire such skills as needed.
Scrum combines four formal events for inspection and adaptation within a containing event, the Sprint. These events work because they implement the empirical Scrum pillars of transparency, inspection, and adaptation.

THE COMPONENTS OF SCRUM

Scrum prescribes:

 3 accounabilities in a Scrum Team - a Product Owner, Developers & a Scrum Master.
 3 artifacts - Product Backlog, Sprint Backlog & Increment.
 5 events - Sprint, Sprint Planning, Daily Scrum, Sprint Review & Sprint Retrospective.

THE PILLARS OF SCRUM

The 3 empirical pillars of Scum are - Transparency, Inspection, and Adaptation.

Transparency

The emergent process and work must be visible to those performing the work as well as those receiving the work. With Scrum, important decisions are based on the perceived state of its three formal artifacts, artifacts that have low transparency can lead to decisions that diminish value and increase risk. Transparency enables inspection; inspection without transparency is misleading and wasteful.
In Scrum, transparency is implemented in the Artifacts and having the Scrum Events to help people to be aware of what is going on.

Inspection

Inspection characterizes the empirical Scrum nature of testing hypotheses. It is about planning and trying something, checking what happens, learning from it, and adapting. The Scrum artifacts and the progress toward agreed goals must be inspected frequently and diligently to detect potentially undesirable variances or problems. To help with inspection, Scrum provides cadence in the form of its five events.
Inspection enables adaptation. Inspection without adaptation is considered pointless. Scrum events are designed to provoke change.

Adaptation

If any aspects of a process deviate outside acceptable limits or if the resulting product is unacceptable, the process being applied or the materials being produced must be adjusted. The adjustment must be made as soon as possible to minimize further deviation.
Adaptation becomes more difficult when the people involved are not empowered or self-managing. A Scrum Team is expected to adapt the moment it learns anything new through inspection.
In Scrum, adaptation occurs throughout the Sprint as needed, not just limited to a specific Scrum event.

SCRUM VALUES

Successful use of Scrum depends on people becoming more proficient in living five values:

 Commitment - The Scrum Team commits to achieving its goals and to supporting each other.
 Focus - The Scrum Team's primary focus is on the work of the Sprint to make the best possible progress toward these goals.
 Openness - The Scrum Team and its stakeholders are open about the work and the challenges.
 Respect - Scrum Team members respect each other to be capable, independent people, and are respected as such by the people with whom they work.
 Courage - The Scrum Team members have the courage to do the right thing, to work on tough problems.

These values give direction to the Scrum Team with regard to their work, actions, and behavior. The decisions that are made, the steps taken, and the way Scrum is used should reinforce these values, not diminish or undermine them. The Scrum Team members learn and explore the values as they work with the Scrum events and artifacts. When these values are embodied by the Scrum Team and the people they work with, the empirical Scrum pillars come to life, building trust.

WHAT HAPPENS IN SCRUM

A Scrum Master fosters an environment where:
  • A Product Owner orders the work for a complex problem into a Product Backlog.
  • The Scrum Team turns a selection of the work into an Increment of value during a Sprint.
  • The Scrum Team, and its stakeholders inspect the results, and adjust for the next Sprint.
  • The next Sprint starts, and the process is repeated.

CHALLENGES IN IMPLEMENTATION

Resistance to change is common when people feel it disrupts their routines responsibilities, or comfort zones.
Introducing Scrum in organizations often presents challenges, as it necessitates a substantial shift in mindset, culture, and operational practices. It means moving from traditional hierarchical structures to self-managing teams and valuing bottom-up intelligence. Moreover, it requires the commitment and support of stakeholders at all organizational levels. Overcoming resistance and fear of change is key to successfully implementing Scrum.

ROLE OF STAKEHOLDERS IN SCRUM

Stakeholders are crucial in Scrum, as they're impacted by the product and invested in its progress. It is essential for them to understand Scrum principles for effective collaboration with the Scrum Team, offer feedback, and make informed choices. This understanding leads to transparency, better communication, and aligned expectations.

ROLE OF MANAGEMENT IN SCRUM

Management external to the Scrum Team doesn't directly participate in product development but plays a crucial role in guiding the organization's vision and strategy. They support bottom-up intelligence and provide the team with insights, resources, and an environment necessary for being efficient. By fostering trust and collaboration, they empower the team to be self-managing, encourage ownership, collective decision-making, and help the team deliver value and improve continuously. By cultivating trust and collaboration, management empowers the team to self-manage, encourages them to take ownership, make collective decisions, and continuously deliver value and improve.

CHANGING SCRUM TERMINOLOGY

Scrum uses specific terminology to define its accountabilities, events, artifacts, and rules. This is to create a common language and understanding within teams, aiding in the adoption of Scrum practices. Organizations may alter this terminology to match their culture or improve comprehension among team members. However, changing the terms without clear communication can hinder actual change and lead to lost benefits. While using familiar language may ease the transition, it's crucial to ensure that the new terms do not cause confusion or miscommunication. Clarity and shared understanding of Scrum concepts are essential for successful implementation.

WHAT IS NOT ALLOWED IN SCRUM

Hardening sprints, integration sprints, release sprints, and sprint zero are not permitted in Scrum, as they contradict the fundamental principles of delivering a valuable Increment each Sprint. These practices may lead to inefficiencies, delays, and a deviation from Scrum's iterative and incremental approach, potentially undermining the core principle of empiricism.

WHERE CAN SCRUM BE APPLIED

Although it was initially designed for software development, it has found applications in other fields such as research, sales, schools, government, marketing, and managing the operation of organizations

END NOTE

According to the Scrum Guide, the Scrum framework is immutable. While implementing only parts of Scrum is possible, the result is not Scrum. Scrum exists only in its entirety and functions well as a container for other techniques, methodologies, and practices.
Scrum is adaptable but not for every problem. It works best in complex environments. It improves predictability but doesn't guarantee on-time delivery due to product development complexities.
While Scrum can speed up delivery, its main focus is continuous delivery of valuable 'Done' Increments. To measure Scrum success, the focus should be on customer value, team improvement, and sustainable delivery, instead of outputs.
