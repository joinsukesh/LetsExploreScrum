SCRUM TEAM

The fundamental unit of Scrum is a small team of people, a Scrum Team.
Scrum defines three specific accountabilities within the Scrum Team: the Developers, the Product Owner, and the Scrum Master.
None of the these accountabilities are necessarily full-time. One person could take on multiple roles or the same role in multiple teams.
Within a Scrum Team, there are no sub-teams or hierarchies. It is a cohesive unit of professionals focused on one objective at a time, the Product Goal.

SIZE OF A SCRUM TEAM

The Scrum Guide does not define a specific size for Scrum Teams. Instead, it suggests that a Scrum Team should be small enough to remain nimble and large enough to complete significant work within a Sprint, typically 10 or fewer people.
The size may vary depending on the nature of the work, complexity of the product, and the required skills and expertise.
In general, it is observed that smaller teams communicate better, and are more productive.
If Scrum Teams become too large, they should consider reorganizing into multiple cohesive Scrum Teams, each focused on the same product. Therefore, they should share the same Product Goal, Product Backlog, and Product Owner.

RESPONSIBILITIES OF A SCRUM TEAM

The Scrum Team is responsible for all product-related activities from stakeholder collaboration, verification, maintenance, operation, experimentation, research and development, and anything else that might be required.
The team works in Sprints at a sustainable pace, which improves its focus and consistency.
The entire Scrum Team is accountable for creating a valuable, useful Increment every Sprint.

QUALITIES OF A SCRUM TEAM

Scrum Teams are self-managed, and cross-functional.

 Self-Managed
Being self-managed means that the team has the ability to organize, and regulate its own work without constant external supervision. Self-managed teams take ownership of their tasks, make decisions collectively, and adapt to changing circumstances. They don't rely on a manager to dictate every step; instead, they collaborate and find solutions independently.

Scrum promotes self-management in several ways. One way is by allowing the Scrum Team to decide who does what, when, and how. Another way is by removing titles for Scrum Team members, except for the Product Owner, Scrum Master, and Developers, which encourages collaboration and shared responsibility to make decisions on the best approach to deliver value.

 Cross-Functional
Cross-functional teams possess a diverse set of skills and expertise necessary to deliver a complete product increment.
Rather than having specialized roles (e.g., only developers, only testers), cross-functional teams include members with various skillsâ€”such as development, testing, design, and more. This diversity enables them to handle end-to-end tasks within a Sprint.
Not every member needs to be an expert in every aspect of the development process. Scrum Teams are cross-functional, meaning the members collectively have all the skills necessary to create value each Sprint.

In cases where the problem at hand is too complex for the Developers to figure out on their own, the Scrum Master can collaborate with them, potentially arranging specialized training or having a specialist join the Scrum Team when their expertise is required.

 Trust is essential for Scrum's success. Team members must trust each other to collaborate effectively, deliver value, and make decisions together. Trust creates transparency, open communication, and a safe environment for experimentation, and learning. Without trust, collaboration suffers, and the team's ability to self-organize, and deliver high-quality work may be impacted.

HOW TO FORM A SCRUM TEAM

They are structured, and empowered by the organization to manage their own work. Scrum values self-management, and autonomy.
One way to form Scrum Teams that align with these values is to bring all individuals together, and allow them to self-organize into teams.
Another way is to allow existing teams to suggest how they would like to organize themselves into the new structure.
Both of these methods give individuals a voice in the process, and empower them to take ownership of their work.

HOW SHOULD A SCRUM TEAM GET STARTED

Typically, the Product Owner shares the product vision, goals, and context with the team, and answer any questions they might have. The team ensures that they have a clear and shared understanding of what 'Done' means for their product, investing enough time in creating the Definition of Done.

CHANGES IN MEMBERSHIP

Scrum has no restrictions on changing the Scrum Team's membership, but it can have implications.
Adding new team members can decrease productivity initially due to time required for familiarization. Changing team composition can temporarily disrupt processes, and dynamics.
While productivity may increase in the long term, it's not necessarily linear, as more people can lead to increased communication channels, reducing capacity.
Having stable teams is advantageous, as they are more likely to evolve into high-performing teams, achieve enhanced productivity, and promote continuous improvement over the long term.
However, necessary changes should be made to ensure the team's long-term success, and while doing so, it is wise to consider a short-term reduction in productivity.
